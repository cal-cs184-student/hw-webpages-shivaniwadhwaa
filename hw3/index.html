Overview

In this assignment we built a physically based path tracer from scratch. We started by implementing ray generation and transforming camera rays into world space, then implemented ray-triangle and ray-sphere intersection using Möller-Trumbore and the quadratic formula respectively. To make rendering practical on complex scenes we built a BVH acceleration structure that splits geometry along the longest bounding box axis, bringing render times from minutes down to under a second.

For lighting we implemented a diffuse BSDF and two versions of direct lighting: uniform hemisphere sampling and importance sampling. Importance sampling was much less noisy since every sample pointed directly toward a light source. We then extended this to full global illumination through recursive path tracing, adding Russian Roulette termination to keep the estimator unbiased. Finally we implemented adaptive sampling, which stops sampling a pixel early once a statistical convergence criterion is met, concentrating samples where they are actually needed.

The most interesting thing we learned was how much visual richness comes from indirect lighting. The color bleeding and soft fill light that emerged naturally from the recursive light transport simulation made the scenes look dramatically more realistic than direct lighting alone, without any hand-tuned approximations.


Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection (20 Points)

Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.

To generate a camera ray, we first took the normalized image coordinates (x, y) and figured out where they landed on the virtual sensor plane at z = -1 in camera space. The sensor's bounds were defined by the field of view angles, so we scaled x and y by tan(hFov/2) and tan(vFov/2) to get the actual position on the sensor. The ray direction in camera space then just pointed from the origin through that sensor point. We multiplied by the camera-to-world matrix c2w to rotate the direction into world space, normalized it, and built the ray starting at pos. We also set min_t and max_t to the near and far clip planes so we'd only look at intersections in the valid range.

In raytrace_pixel, we looped ns_aa times, and each iteration we grabbed a random (u, v) offset within the pixel from the grid sampler, normalized those coordinates by the image dimensions, and shot a ray through that point. The offset was sampled uniformly from a unit square, so each ray ended up going through a slightly different point within the pixel rather than always through the center. We called est_radiance_global_illumination on each ray, added up all the results, and divided by the sample count at the end to get the average radiance. This is basically a Monte Carlo estimate of the integral of radiance over the pixel, and the more samples we used the closer we got to the true value. The random jittering within each pixel was also what gave us antialiasing since edges that fell within a pixel would get partially sampled rather than just snapping to one color or the other.

For primitive intersection, every ray got tested against the scene geometry and we only counted it as a hit if t was inside the ray's [min_t, max_t] range. When we did find a valid hit, we updated max_t to that t value so anything farther away down the ray would get skipped automatically. This was important for correctness since without it we could end up reporting intersections behind already-hit surfaces.


Explain the triangle intersection algorithm you implemented in your own words.

We used the Möller-Trumbore algorithm for ray-triangle intersection. The main idea was to solve for the intersection point directly in barycentric coordinates instead of finding the ray-plane intersection first and then checking if that point was actually inside the triangle. This made it more efficient since we could bail out early the moment we knew the intersection was invalid.

We started by computing two edge vectors from p1 to p2 and p1 to p3. Then we computed the determinant using a cross product of the ray direction and the second edge dotted with the first edge. If the determinant was close to zero, the ray was pretty much parallel to the triangle so we just returned false right away since there couldn't be a valid intersection.

From there we solved for u and v one at a time using cross and dot products, bailing out early if either one fell outside [0, 1] or if u + v went over 1, since that meant the hit point was outside the triangle's boundaries. The barycentric coordinates had to satisfy u ≥ 0, v ≥ 0, and u + v ≤ 1 for the point to actually be inside the triangle, so we checked each condition as we went and returned false as soon as any of them failed. Then we solved for t and checked it was within [min_t, max_t]. If everything checked out we had a valid intersection and updated max_t to t.

In the full intersect function we did all of the same steps but also computed w = 1 - u - v and used the three barycentric weights to interpolate between the vertex normals n1, n2, n3 to get a smooth surface normal at the hit point. This gave us per-pixel normal interpolation across the triangle surface rather than a flat constant normal, which is what makes curved surfaces look smooth even though they're made of flat triangles. We then filled in the rest of the Intersection struct with t, the primitive pointer, and the bsdf so the rest of the pipeline had what it needed to shade the surface correctly.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy (20 Points)

Walk through your BVH construction algorithm. Explain the heuristic you chose for picking the splitting point.

To construct the BVH, we first computed a bounding box over all the primitives in the current range and created a new node with that box. If the number of primitives was less than or equal to max_leaf_size, we just made it a leaf node and returned. Otherwise we needed to split the primitives into two groups to recurse on.

For the splitting heuristic, we picked the axis with the longest extent of the bounding box, since splitting along the widest dimension tends to produce a more balanced tree. We then computed the average centroid of all the primitives along that axis and used it as the split point. We used std::partition to rearrange the primitives in place so everything whose centroid was below the average went to the left child and everything above went to the right. If all primitives ended up on one side (which would cause infinite recursion), we just forced a split down the middle by count. We then recursively called construct_bvh on the two halves to build out the rest of the tree.

Part 3: Direct Illumination (20 Points)

Walk through both implementations of the direct lighting function.

For hemisphere sampling, we looped over num_samples directions uniformly sampled on the hemisphere around the hit point. For each sampled direction we cast a shadow ray from the hit point out into the scene, and if it happened to hit something emissive we added its contribution using the reflection equation: f(wo, wi) * emission * cos(θ) * 2π, where the 2π comes from dividing by the pdf of uniform hemisphere sampling which is 1/(2π). We averaged all the samples at the end.

For importance sampling, instead of sampling random directions we looped directly over every light in the scene. For each light we called sample_L to get the direction toward the light, the distance to it, and the pdf of that sample. We then checked if the light was behind the surface by looking at the z component of the incoming direction in object space, and skipped it if so. If the light was in front, we cast a shadow ray with max_t set to just before the light so we only detected occluders between the hit point and the light. If nothing blocked it we added the contribution using the same reflection equation but divided by the pdf from sample_L instead. For point lights we only took one sample since they always return the same value.

Compare the results between uniform hemisphere sampling and lighting sampling in a one-paragraph analysis.

Uniform hemisphere sampling and importance sampling both estimate the same direct lighting integral but with very different noise levels. With hemisphere sampling, we sampled random directions on the hemisphere and only got a useful signal when one of those directions happened to point toward a light, which is rare when the light is small relative to the hemisphere. This led to very grainy results, especially in the soft shadow regions where only some rays reached the light. With importance sampling, every sample was guaranteed to point toward a light source, so all of them contributed meaningful information. This made the soft shadows much cleaner with the same number of samples. Looking at the -l comparison, we could also see that increasing the number of light rays from 1 to 64 steadily reduced the noise in the soft shadows under the bunny, since more samples per light gave a better estimate of how much of the light was actually visible from each point. At -l 1 the shadows were very speckled and grainy, but by -l 64 they were smooth and well-defined.


Part 4: Global Illumination (20 Points)

Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.

To implement indirect lighting, we built on top of the direct lighting from Part 3. In at_least_one_bounce_radiance, we first added the direct one bounce radiance if isAccumBounces was true or if we were exactly at max_ray_depth. We then checked if the ray depth had reached 1 and returned early if so. For Russian Roulette, we randomly terminated with a probability of 0.35 using coin_flip. If we didn't terminate, we sampled a new incoming direction using the BSDF's sample_f, transformed it to world space, and cast a new ray from the hit point with depth decremented by 1. If that ray hit something, we recursively called at_least_one_bounce_radiance on the new intersection and added its contribution weighted by f * cos_theta / pdf / (1 - term_prob) to account for the BSDF, the cosine falloff, the sampling pdf, and the Russian Roulette continuation probability. In est_radiance_global_illumination we then returned the sum of zero_bounce_radiance and at_least_one_bounce_radiance.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling (20 Points)

Explain adaptive sampling. Walk through your implementation of the adaptive sampling.

Adaptive sampling is a technique that lets us avoid wasting samples on pixels that have already converged. Instead of always tracing a fixed number of rays through every pixel, we trace rays until we're statistically confident that adding more samples won't meaningfully change the result. This way we spend more samples on difficult pixels like edges and shadow boundaries, and fewer on simple pixels like flat walls that converge quickly.

To check for convergence we tracked two running statistics for each pixel: s1, the sum of illuminance values across all samples so far, and s2, the sum of squared illuminance values. Every samplesPerBatch samples we used these to compute the mean μ = s1/n and variance σ² = (1/(n-1)) * (s2 - s1²/n). We then computed the convergence measure I = 1.96 * σ / √n, which represents a 95% confidence interval for the true mean. If I was less than or equal to maxTolerance * μ we stopped sampling that pixel early since we were confident it had converged. If not we kept going until we either hit the convergence condition or reached the maximum sample count ns_aa. At the end we updated sampleCountBuffer with the actual number of samples taken rather than the maximum, which lets us generate the sampling rate visualization image that shows which parts of the scene required the most samples.
